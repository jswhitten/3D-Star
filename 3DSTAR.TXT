                                3 D - S T A R
                                Version  0.95

INTRODUCTION  
  
  3DSTAR is a program designed to show nearby stars from different viewing
perspectives.  We on Earth are only able to see the stars from one place,
Earth, which doesn't give us much perspective on how far away different
stars are, or how bright they really are.  This program allows you to
travel through this part of the galaxy, exploring our neighborhood of space
out to a few thousand light-years.  Some of the features of this version
are:

ţ Actual (X,Y,Z) galactic coordinates of 357 stars, including:  
    The 25 brightest stars (as seen from Earth)
    The 26 nearest stars to Earth (including Sol)
    The brighter stars of every major constellation

ţ Add more stars with the program 3DDATA.BAS, using their equatorial
  coordinates (RA, DEC), distance in light-years, absolute magnitude,
  and spectral class.

ţ Apparent magnitudes calculated as you travel through space, so you
  can see the stars' correct brightness relative to each other.

ţ Choose any starting location measured from (0,0,0) at Sol, and any
  course from Galactic Longitude 0ř to 360ř and from Galactic Latitude
  -90ř to +90ř.

ţ Choose any speed, even millions or billions of times the speed of light.

ţ VGA 640x480 16-color graphics, with the palette altered for better
  simulation of spectral colors.

ţ Information bar displays direction and distance info on any star.


  You should have the following files:

3DSTAR.DOC              This documentation
3DSTAR.EXE              The main program
3DDATA.EXE              Enter stars into the database
3DSTAR.DAT              The star database

  If you have ordered the source code, it should include these files:

3DSTAR.BAS
3DDATA.BAS


GETTING STARTED

  Type "3DSTAR" to start the program.  When the program asks you how many 
stars to load, hit <ENTER> to load all of them.  After loading the stars 
from the 3DSTAR.DAT file into memory, the program will ask you for starting
parameters.  The first time you run the program, hit <ENTER> eight times 
to accept the default values.

  The top part of the screen will display the stars, and the lower part has
the current status.  At Magnification = 1, the screen goes from -180ř on the
left side of the screen to +180ř on the right side of the screen.  The
center of the screen is at 0ř (straight ahead).  In the up-down direction,
+90ř is at the top of the screen and -90ř is at the bottom.  If you were in
a spaceship looking straight out the right window, for example, you would
be looking 90ř which is exactly 3/4 of the screen from the left side.  If
you were looking out the left window, you would be facing -90ř (or 270ř)
and that is 1/4 of the screen from the left side.  For a more realistic
view of just straight ahead, you may want to increase the magnification
to 4 or so, which will show you the area of the sky from 315ř to 45ř 
(left-right) and from -22.5ř to +22.5ř (up-down).  But for now, leave it 
on magnification 1 so you can see the whole sky at once.

  You are heading in the direction 0ř mark 0ř (see the "Course" display), 
which is directly towards the Galactic Core.  You are at coordinates
[0, 0, 0], which is Sol, our Sun's, coordinates.  The yellow star just to
the right of center is Alpha Centauri, closest star to Sol at only 4.3
light-years.  The bright white star on the right side of the screen, a
little below the Galactic plane, is Sirius.  The constellation to the right
of Sirius is Orion.  Up in the upper-left corner of the screen is the
Big Dipper.  Its shape is a little distorted because it is near the edge
of a spherical map plotted on a flat screen, the same reason Greenland
looks so big on flat maps.  About a fourth of the screen from the left
side (that's 90ř to the left) is a triangle of three bright white stars.
Those stars are Deneb, Vega, and Altair, and they are known as the
"Summer Triangle" because they are high above the horizon on summer
evenings in the northern hemisphere.

  If you press capital "A" (for accelerate) you will start moving.  Since you
are moving directly away from the Sun now, it is behind you and you can't
see it unless you turn slightly, even with a 360-degree view.  Alpha Centauri 
is slowly moving away from the center, passing the blue star Beta Centauri, 
then the two stars Acrux and Beta Crucis in the Southern Cross.

  Capital "M" will toggle the Star Map on and off.  This shows you a view
from above your location ("up" in the Z direction) with the stars' brightness
depending on their absolute (real) magnitude rather than their apparent
brightness from your location.  You can move your ship around in this mode,
just like normal, and your location is the small cyan circle in the middle
of the screen.  The magnification now controls how many pixels for each
light-year, so at magnification = 1, 1 pixel is 1 light year.

  Here are the keys that are currently available while the program is
running:

  Z ........... Zoom in by 2x
  z ........... UnZoom by 1/2x
  A ........... Speed up (or Accelerate)
  a ........... Slow down
  2, 4, 6, 8 .. turn ship by 5ř in that direction; use the keypad with
                Num Lock on
  5 ........... Set a course for the star on info display.  You may need
                to press 5 several times for a perfect aim; check the info
                bar to see if the course is correct.
  N ........... Next star on information display
  n ........... Last star on information display
  M ........... Toggle Star Map On/Off
  Q or ^Break . Quit

  On slower computers (anything less than a 486DX) this program is likely
to run slowly with all 357 of the stars loaded.  Look at the "System Speed"
indicator at the bottom of the screen; it tells you how many seconds it 
takes between each update of the screen.  If it's more than 1 or 2 seonds,
the program will respond too slowly to commands and you may wish to restart
the progam and load less stars.  At the prompt "How many stars?" type a
number less than 357.  The less stars you load, the less detailed the
starfield will be but the faster it will run.  If you just want to see the
constellations from a different star, and you're not going to be moving,
load all the stars for maximum detail.


HISTORY

Early 1992 ......... Started writing simple 3DSTAR program, with random stars
                     and no differences in brightness, color, etc.  Problem
                     with keeping stars in the right quadrants halted
                     progress.

December 28, 1992 .. Started working on it again.  Also started trying to
                     figure out how to translate spherical coordinates from
                     one system to another, for 3DSTAR to translate to the
                     observers coordinates and viewing angle, and also for 
                     3DDATA to convert from equatorial to galactic coords.

December 30, 1992 .. Derived spherical translation equations from 2-d polar
                     coordinate system rotation equations and got both
                     3DSTAR and 3DDATA working, but without magnitudes and
                     spectral types.

December 31, 1993 .. Started working on apparent magnitudes, had problem
                     with stars leaving glitches on the screen after they
                     move.

January 2, 1994 .... Finished version 0.01, 3DSTAR now works at any viewing
                     angle, from any point in space.  Spectral types and
                     apparent magnitudes working perfectly, with no screen
                     glitches.

January 30, 1994 ... Finished version 0.90, compiled with QuickBasic 4.5.
                     The StarMap still is not finished but the palette has
                     been adjusted (no more brown or green stars!) and the
                     stars are now points instead of disks.  Looks much
                     better and runs much faster than the QBasic interpreted
                     program did, and the source code is now structured 
                     with subprograms and functions for easier modification.

February 2, 1994 ... Finished version 0.91, the database now has 333 stars
                     (0.90 only had 63).  Information bar added.

February 4, 1994 ... Finished version 0.92, now capable of setting a course
                     to the selected star, and displays the apparent
                     magnitude of that star from your present location.  The
                     menu now asks for a starting location, so you can enter
                     a star's name and start the program at those 
                     coordinates.

February 21, 1994 .. Finished version 0.93, now 357 stars in database.

May 19, 1994 ....... First version released (version 0.94).  Added feature
                     to set number of stars to be loaded for slower 
                     computers.

June 4, 1994 ....... Version 0.95 released.  Now has a StarMap mode.
